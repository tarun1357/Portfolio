---
title: "Reliable hot paths under variable load"
date: "2026-03-14"
description: "Notes on profiling, caching, and protecting databases when spikes are normal—not exceptional."
---

Most backend incidents look boring in hindsight: a queue backed up, a cache turned into a RPC fan-out, or a query plan flipped because statistics drifted.

When traffic is spikey, I reach for three anchors:

1. **Measure before reshaping** — `pprof` (or your runtime’s equivalent), slow query logs, and saturation signals tell you whether you’re CPU-bound, IO-bound, or coordination-bound.

2. **Caches need an invalidation story** — TTL alone is not a design; neither is “we’ll purge manually.” Pair caches with explicit domains and metrics that prove hit ratio *and* staleness tolerance.

3. **Protect the database** — bulkheads: rate limits, bounded pools, batching, and sometimes dropping work deliberately rather than taking everything down.

These are boring tools. That’s why they work in production.
